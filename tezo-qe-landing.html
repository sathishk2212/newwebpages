The real causes

In what ways is QE actually slowing you down?

Three patterns show up in almost every assessment we run. None of them are fixed by adding more testers.

/ 01

AI coding outpaced the safety net

Developers now ship far more code with assistants, but QA capacity stayed flat. Review and regression became the line everything queues behind, so the speed you gained upstream is lost at the gate.

30 to 50% more code, same QA capacity
/ 02

Broken automation that still depends on too many people

Brittle scripts break on every UI change. Teams burn most of their automation effort keeping the suite alive instead of growing coverage. It takes a crowd to run what should run itself.

35 to 45% of automation time on maintenance
/ 03

No CI/CD-native testing, no shift-left

Quality sits at the end of the pipeline instead of inside it. Defects surface late, at the most expensive moment to fix, because testing was never wired into the build and the team never moved left.

Defects found late cost the most to fix
What "AI-augmented" actually means

The augmentation is specific, not a slogan.

We apply AI in the three places it measurably compresses QE work. Everywhere else, experienced engineers still make the call.

Tests generated from user stories

Requirements and acceptance criteria become draft test cases automatically, so coverage tracks the backlog instead of lagging quarters behind it.

Cuts authoring time, lifts coverage

Self-healing automation

Locators that adapt when the UI shifts, so a renamed element does not break the suite. Maintenance stops eating the time meant for new coverage.

Cuts the maintenance tax

Risk-based test selection

Change analysis runs the tests most likely to catch a regression first, so a fraction of the suite protects the release without the full run.

How we solve it / the assessment

A two to three week assessment. Same shape every time, tuned to the question that matters most for your role.

Your lens We run the engagement against your DORA metrics and AI-coding adoption timeline. The synthesis ranks initiatives by their effect on change failure rate first.
DAY 01

Kickoff and access

A two-hour working session with your QA, engineering, and product leads. We name the three things this assessment has to answer.

→ Scope brief signed
DAYS 02 TO 05

Coverage instrumentation

We map your existing test estate against business-critical paths. Static analysis on the suite, plus conversations with the people who maintain it.

→ Coverage heat-map
DAYS 06 TO 09

Defect leakage and DORA correlation

Six months of production defects pulled, classified, and traced. Then correlated to your AI-tooling adoption date so the cause and effect is visible, not assumed.

→ Leakage report
DAYS 10 TO 12

Roadmap synthesis

Twelve candidate initiatives, modeled for time, cost, and outcome. Ranked by ROI. The ones we would skip, called out by name.

→ Initiative ledger
DAYS 13 TO 14

Pressure test

We walk the findings past your engineering leads. Anything that does not survive their pushback gets cut or rewritten before the readout.

→ Revised draft
DAY 15

Readout and handoff

A ninety-minute readout with the team you want in the room. Final deck, raw artifacts, recordings. Yours to execute with us or without.

→ Everything you paid for
